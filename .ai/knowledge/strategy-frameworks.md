# Strategy Frameworks — Compendium

Practical reference for strategic analysis and business model design. Each framework is presented with its purpose, mechanics, inputs, outputs, limitations, and when to use it. Enables the agent and user to select the right strategic tool for the situation and apply it correctly.

## When to use

- Analyzing competitive landscape or market entry decisions (`market-research`)
- Evaluating or designing business models (`hypothesis-testing`, `mvp-development`)
- Assessing stakeholder dynamics and strategic positioning (`stakeholder-analysis`)
- Calculating return on investment or comparing strategic options (`roi-calculation`)
- Building or refining value propositions
- Strategic planning sessions and investor presentations (`presentation-design`)

## 1. SWOT Analysis

### Purpose

Assess internal strengths/weaknesses and external opportunities/threats to inform strategic decisions.

### Mechanics

| Dimension | Focus | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Strengths** (internal) | What the organization does well | Technology, team, brand, IP, partnerships |
| **Weaknesses** (internal) | Where the organization underperforms | Gaps in skills, resources, reputation |
| **Opportunities** (external) | Favorable external conditions | Market trends, regulation changes, unserved segments |
| **Threats** (external) | External risks | Competitors, disruption, economic shifts |

### How to apply

1. Assemble cross-functional team (diverse perspectives reduce blind spots)
2. Brainstorm each quadrant with evidence, not opinions
3. Prioritize items by impact and likelihood
4. Generate strategic actions: leverage S+O, address W+T, mitigate S+T, overcome W+O

### Limitations

- Static snapshot — does not model dynamics or evolution
- Risk of confirmation bias if done solo
- No inherent prioritization mechanism — all items appear equal
- Works best as a starting point, not a standalone strategy tool

### Best for

Quick situational assessment, kickoff discussions, stakeholder alignment, identifying areas for deeper analysis.

## 2. Porter's Five Forces

### Purpose

Analyze industry structure to assess competitive intensity and long-term profitability potential.

### The five forces

| Force | Question | High pressure means |
|---|---|---|
| **Threat of new entrants** | How easy is it for new competitors to enter? | Low barriers → more competition → lower margins |
| **Bargaining power of suppliers** | Can suppliers dictate terms? | Concentrated suppliers → higher input costs |
| **Bargaining power of buyers** | Can customers drive prices down? | Concentrated buyers or low switching costs → margin pressure |
| **Threat of substitutes** | Can customers satisfy the same need differently? | Viable alternatives → price ceiling |
| **Competitive rivalry** | How intense is competition among existing firms? | Many similar competitors → price wars → lower profitability |

### How to apply

1. Define the industry boundary precisely (too broad = meaningless)
2. Identify key players for each force (specific companies, not abstractions)
3. Rate each force (low / medium / high) with evidence
4. Map strategic implications: where to compete, how to build defensibility
5. Identify which forces you can influence vs which you must accept

### Limitations

- Industry-level, not company-level — does not model individual firm capabilities
- Assumes relatively stable industry boundaries (weaker for platform/ecosystem competition)
- Does not capture complementors (Brandenburger & Nalebuff's "sixth force")
- Snapshot analysis — combine with dynamic tools like Wardley Maps

### Best for

Market entry decisions, competitive positioning, investor due diligence, industry attractiveness assessment.

## 3. Blue Ocean Strategy

### Purpose

Create uncontested market space by simultaneously pursuing differentiation and low cost through value innovation.

### Core concept: value innovation

Traditional strategy assumes a trade-off between differentiation (more value, higher cost) and cost leadership (less value, lower cost). Blue Ocean Strategy breaks this trade-off by **simultaneously** raising value and lowering cost.

### The ERRC framework (Four Actions)

| Action | Question | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Eliminate** | Which factors should be removed entirely? | Reduces cost and complexity |
| **Reduce** | Which factors should fall below industry standard? | Cuts overserving and overdesign |
| **Raise** | Which factors should exceed industry standard? | Addresses buyer compromises |
| **Create** | Which factors should be introduced for the first time? | Opens new demand |

Eliminate + Reduce → lowers cost structure.
Raise + Create → increases buyer value.

### Strategy canvas

Visual tool plotting the industry's competitive factors (x-axis) against offering level (y-axis). Each competitor's value curve shows their strategic profile. A Blue Ocean strategy produces a **divergent** value curve — visually distinct from existing competitors.

### How to apply

1. Map the current strategy canvas for your industry
2. Apply all four ERRC actions (not just Raise and Create)
3. Draw the new value curve — it should diverge, focus, and have a compelling tagline
4. Test with non-customers: why do they not use existing offerings?
5. Validate commercially before scaling

### Limitations

- Blue Oceans attract imitators — sustainability requires continuous innovation
- Framework is better at generating strategic ideas than evaluating feasibility
- Can underestimate execution difficulty
- Not all industries have accessible Blue Ocean potential

### Best for

Product differentiation strategy, new market creation, breaking out of commoditized competition, strategic brainstorming.

## 4. Business Model Canvas (BMC)

### Purpose

Map how a business creates, delivers, and captures value on a single page.

### The 9 building blocks

| # | Block | Question |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Customer Segments** | Who do we serve? |
| 2 | **Value Propositions** | Why do customers choose us? |
| 3 | **Channels** | How do we reach and deliver to customers? |
| 4 | **Customer Relationships** | What type of relationship does each segment expect? |
| 5 | **Revenue Streams** | How do we make money? |
| 6 | **Key Resources** | What do we need to operate? |
| 7 | **Key Activities** | What must we do to deliver value? |
| 8 | **Key Partnerships** | Who do we rely on externally? |
| 9 | **Cost Structure** | What does it cost to operate? |

Right side = value creation and delivery (market-facing). Left side = value production (operational). Bottom = financial viability.

### How to apply

1. Start with ONE customer segment — resist the urge to describe all segments at once
2. Clearly articulate the value proposition for that segment
3. Map channels, relationships, and revenue streams for that segment
4. Then map the operational side: activities, resources, partnerships
5. Estimate cost structure
6. Test assumptions — every block is a hypothesis until validated

### Limitations

- Descriptive, not prescriptive — does not tell you *what* to do, only how to describe what you plan
- Does not model competition or external environment (combine with Porter or SWOT)
- Can become a one-time exercise if not revisited as assumptions change
- Weak on competitive advantage and moat analysis

### Best for

Business model design and communication, team alignment, investor presentations, strategic pivots, comparing business model alternatives.

## 5. Lean Canvas

### Purpose

Startup-focused adaptation of BMC that prioritizes risk and uncertainty over operational detail.

### The 9 blocks (differences from BMC highlighted)

| # | Lean Canvas block | Replaces (BMC) | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Problem** (top 3) | — | What pains does the customer have? |
| 2 | **Customer Segments** | Same | Who has this problem most acutely? |
| 3 | **Unique Value Proposition** | Value Propositions | Single clear message of why you're different |
| 4 | **Solution** (top 3 features) | — | How do you solve the top problems? |
| 5 | **Channels** | Same | How do you reach customers? |
| 6 | **Revenue Streams** | Same | How do you make money? |
| 7 | **Cost Structure** | Same | What does it cost? |
| 8 | **Key Metrics** | Key Resources | Numbers that show if the idea works |
| 9 | **Unfair Advantage** | Key Partnerships | What can't be easily copied? |

### BMC vs Lean Canvas — when to use which

| Dimension | BMC | Lean Canvas |
|---|---|---|
| Stage | Established business, growth | Early-stage, pre-product-market fit |
| Focus | Comprehensive business model | Riskiest assumptions |
| Strength | Holistic view, team alignment | Speed, hypothesis-driven |
| Weakness | Can obscure risk | Less useful for operational planning |

### Best for

Early-stage startups, idea validation, accelerator applications, rapid iteration on business model hypotheses.

## 6. Value Proposition Canvas

### Purpose

Zoom into the fit between one customer segment's needs and one value proposition. A detailed companion to the BMC/Lean Canvas value proposition block.

### Two sides

**Customer profile:**
- **Jobs-to-be-done** — tasks, goals, problems the customer tries to accomplish
- **Pains** — obstacles, risks, frustrations when doing the job
- **Gains** — outcomes, benefits the customer desires

**Value map:**
- **Products & services** — what you offer
- **Pain relievers** — how you address specific pains
- **Gain creators** — how you produce specific gains

### Fit

Product-market fit occurs when pain relievers and gain creators match the most important pains and gains from the customer profile. Not every pain needs a reliever — focus on the ones that matter most.

### How to apply

1. Fill in the customer profile first — based on evidence (interviews, data), not assumptions
2. Then map your value map against it
3. Identify gaps: important pains with no reliever, important gains with no creator
4. Iterate: adjust offering or find a different segment

### Best for

Value proposition design, customer interview preparation, feature prioritization, marketing messaging, combined with JTBD analysis (see `jtbd-custdev.md`).

## 7. Wardley Maps

### Purpose

Visualize the strategic landscape by mapping the value chain against component evolution. Provides **situational awareness** — understanding where things are and where they're moving — before making strategic decisions.

### Core concepts

**Value chain (y-axis):** vertical dependency from user need (top) down through components that fulfill it (bottom). What the user sees is at the top; infrastructure is at the bottom.

**Evolution (x-axis):** every component evolves through four stages:

| Stage | Characteristics | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Genesis** | Novel, uncertain, high failure rate | Explore, experiment, tolerate failure |
| **Custom-built** | Growing, scarce, competitive advantage | Build proprietary capability |
| **Product** | Stable, well-defined, multiple providers | Buy, integrate, differentiate on usage |
| **Commodity/utility** | Ubiquitous, standardized, utility | Outsource, consume as service, optimize cost |

### How to create a Wardley Map

1. Identify the user and their need (anchor)
2. Map the value chain: what components are needed to fulfill the need?
3. Position each component on the evolution axis based on current state
4. Draw dependencies between components
5. Identify movement: which components are evolving? what inertia exists?
6. Apply strategic patterns: build vs buy vs outsource; where to invest vs divest

### Key strategic patterns

- **Pioneer-Settler-Town Planner** — different organizational cultures for different evolution stages
- **Inertia** — resistance to evolution driven by past success or sunk cost
- **Componentization** — as things evolve, they become components for higher-order systems
- **Commoditization of means** — yesterday's competitive advantage becomes tomorrow's utility

### Limitations

- Requires practice to create accurate maps
- Positioning on the evolution axis is subjective
- Not widely known — explaining to stakeholders takes effort
- Better for technology-heavy environments; less intuitive for pure-service businesses

### Best for

Technology strategy, build-vs-buy decisions, identifying strategic investment areas, understanding competitive dynamics over time, architecture planning (see `software-architecture-patterns.md`).

## Framework selection guide

| Situation | Recommended framework(s) |
|---|---|
| "What industry are we in and how attractive is it?" | Porter's Five Forces |
| "What are our strengths and where are we vulnerable?" | SWOT |
| "How do we differentiate from competitors?" | Blue Ocean (ERRC) + Strategy Canvas |
| "How does our business model work?" | BMC (established) or Lean Canvas (startup) |
| "Does our product match what customers actually need?" | Value Proposition Canvas + JTBD (`jtbd-custdev.md`) |
| "Where should we invest in technology?" | Wardley Maps |
| "Should we enter this market?" | Porter + SWOT + BMC |
| "How do we pitch to investors?" | Lean Canvas + Value Proposition Canvas |
| "Where is the industry heading?" | Wardley Maps + Porter (trends) |
| "Why are customers not buying?" | Value Proposition Canvas + Forces of Progress (`jtbd-custdev.md`) |

## Decision heuristics

- **Multiple frameworks > one framework** — no single tool gives complete picture; combine 2–3 for depth
- **Start with the customer** — Value Proposition Canvas or JTBD before industry analysis prevents solution-first bias
- **Match tool to maturity** — Lean Canvas for pre-PMF, BMC for post-PMF, Porter for market entry, Wardley for technology strategy
- **Evidence over opinion** — every cell in a canvas, every force in Porter is a hypothesis. Mark confidence level. Validate
- **Revisit regularly** — strategy frameworks are living documents, not one-time artifacts. Markets change, assumptions expire
- **Right granularity** — too broad = useless ("our segment is everyone"); too narrow = missing the market

## Anti-patterns

- **Framework tourism** — using every framework on every problem without depth in any; diminishing returns
- **Canvas-as-paperwork** — filling in canvases to satisfy a process requirement without genuine strategic thinking
- **Static Five Forces** — treating industry structure as permanent; industries are disrupted, forces shift
- **SWOT laundry list** — listing dozens of items per quadrant without prioritization or action
- **Blue Ocean fantasy** — assuming a Blue Ocean exists because you haven't found competitors; sometimes the market is empty for a reason
- **Wardley without action** — creating beautiful maps without using them to make build/buy/partner decisions
- **Single-segment blindness** — building an entire strategy on one customer segment without testing alternatives
