# Sales Methodologies — Practical Reference

Practical guide to major B2B sales methodologies, qualification frameworks, and pipeline management. Enables the agent and user to select the right sales approach, qualify opportunities rigorously, and manage pipeline health with appropriate metrics.

## When to use

- Building or refining sales processes (`sales-segmentation`)
- Preparing for customer or partner conversations
- Qualifying deal opportunities and assessing pipeline
- Designing sales training or interview processes
- Analyzing conversion rates and forecasting revenue (`roi-calculation`)
- Connecting sales practices with customer discovery (see `.vaibe/skills/jtbd-custdev/SKILL.md`)

## 1. SPIN Selling (Rackham)

### Core idea

Guide prospects to discover their own need through structured questioning. Based on research analyzing 35,000+ sales calls. Particularly effective for complex, high-value sales where the buyer's decision process is multi-step.

### The four question types

| Type | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Situation** | Gather facts about the customer's current state | "How do you currently manage project scheduling across teams?" |
| **Problem** | Reveal difficulties, dissatisfactions, challenges | "What happens when priorities conflict between projects?" |
| **Implication** | Explore consequences and costs of the problem | "How does that scheduling conflict affect your quarterly delivery targets?" |
| **Need-Payoff** | Lead customer to articulate the value of solving the problem | "If you could resolve those conflicts automatically, how would that impact your team's output?" |

### Dynamics

- **S → P:** Move quickly from situation to problems; too many situation questions bore prospects
- **P → I:** Implication questions create urgency by making the cost of inaction visible
- **I → N:** Need-Payoff questions let the buyer sell the solution to themselves
- Effective for complex sales where the buyer must justify the purchase internally

### When to use

Large deal sizes, long sales cycles, multiple stakeholders, consultative selling. The customer needs to understand why change is necessary before hearing about solutions.

## 2. Challenger Sale (Dixon & Adamson)

### Core idea

Based on research with 6,000 sales reps across 90 companies. Identified five seller profiles; **Challengers** dramatically outperform in complex B2B sales. 53% of customer loyalty comes from insight-led sales experiences, not relationships.

### The five profiles

| Profile | Characteristic | Performance |
|---|---|---|
| **Challenger** | Teaches, tailors, takes control | Highest performer in complex sales |
| Relationship Builder | Builds rapport and trust | Average; least effective in complex sales |
| Hard Worker | Outworks everyone | Consistent but not top |
| Lone Wolf | Self-directed, rule-breaking | High individual but unscalable |
| Problem Solver | Detail-oriented, reliable | Good for implementation, not deal creation |

### Three pillars

**Teach:** Bring new insights that challenge the customer's assumptions. Don't lead with what you sell — lead with what you know. Commercial teaching reframes how the customer thinks about their business.

**Tailor:** Adapt the message to specific stakeholders. CFO cares about cost reduction; CTO cares about technical debt; CEO cares about market position. Same insight, different framing.

**Take Control:** Constructively push back on objections. Maintain momentum toward a decision. Comfortable discussing money early. Proposes next steps rather than waiting.

### When to use

Complex B2B sales where the customer is well-informed and has access to alternatives. Commoditized markets where relationships alone don't differentiate. Enterprise sales where buying committees need to be educated about problems they don't yet recognize.

## 3. MEDDPICC

### Core idea

Deal qualification framework that surfaces critical intelligence early, preventing late-stage surprises. Organizations using MEDDPICC report ~18% higher win rates and ~24% larger deal sizes. Extended from the original MEDDIC framework with the addition of Paper Process and Competition.

### The eight elements

| Letter | Element | Key question | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| **M** | Metrics | What quantifiable outcomes does the buyer expect? | Grounds value in the buyer's numbers, not your features |
| **E** | Economic Buyer | Who has final budget authority? | Deals stall or die without EB access |
| **D** | Decision Criteria | What factors will the buyer evaluate? | Allows positioning against criteria you can win on |
| **D** | Decision Process | What steps lead to a purchase decision? | Prevents surprises about approvals, committees, timelines |
| **P** | Paper Process | What procurement, legal, and security steps are required? | Longest hidden delay in enterprise sales |
| **I** | Implicate the Pain | How does the problem impact the business quantifiably? | Connects pain to urgency and budget justification |
| **C** | Champion | Who sells your solution internally? | Without a champion, you are marketing, not selling |
| **C** | Competition | Who else is competing for this budget? | Includes direct competitors, alternatives, and "do nothing" |

### Champion qualification

A real champion must have: **power** (influence in the organization), **access** (can connect you to the economic buyer), **vested interest** (personally benefits from the solution succeeding), and **credibility** (trusted internally). A friendly contact without these qualities is a coach, not a champion.

### When to use

Enterprise B2B sales with deal sizes > $50K, long sales cycles, multiple stakeholders, competitive situations. Essential for forecast accuracy — a deal without all eight elements qualified is at risk.

## 4. Sandler Selling System

### Core idea

Buyer and seller are equals. No chasing, no convincing, no pressure. The sales process is structured as a "submarine" — compartments that must be sealed before moving forward. Pioneered the concept of "up-front contracts" (mutual agreements about what happens next).

### The seven steps (Submarine)

| Stage | Step | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Relationship** | 1. Bonding & Rapport | Build genuine human connection |
| | 2. Up-Front Contracts | Agree on agenda, time, outcomes, and next steps before each meeting |
| **Qualification** | 3. Pain | Uncover real business and personal pain using the Pain Funnel |
| | 4. Budget | Qualify whether the prospect has resources (and willingness) to invest |
| | 5. Decision | Map the decision-making process, timeline, and stakeholders |
| **Closing** | 6. Fulfillment | Present the solution only after pain, budget, and decision are qualified |
| | 7. Post-Sell | Reinforce the decision, prevent buyer's remorse, set up implementation |

### The Pain Funnel

Layered questioning that deepens understanding of pain:

1. **Surface level** — "What's happening?" (symptoms)
2. **Business impact** — "How is this affecting operations/revenue?" (cost)
3. **Personal impact** — "How does this affect you personally?" (emotional driver)

The deeper the pain, the stronger the motivation to buy. No pain = no sale.

### When to use

Consultative sales where long-term relationships matter. Effective for preventing "think it over" stalls. Strong in environments where the seller needs to qualify out early to focus resources.

## 5. Solution Selling (Bosworth)

### Core idea

Sales rep as trusted advisor. Diagnose the customer's situation, co-create a vision of the solution, and anchor the decision to measurable value. Customer-centric, not product-centric.

### Key concepts

**Pain Chain:** Maps interconnected problems across the organization. Solving one pain point may affect others — show the systemic impact.

**Vision of Solution:** Co-create a future state with the prospect. Describe capabilities and outcomes, not features. The customer should be able to see themselves in the solution.

**Three question types:**
1. **Open** — explore the situation broadly
2. **Control** — narrow to specific issues
3. **Confirm** — verify understanding and agreement

### Process flow

1. Diagnose pain points and root causes
2. Explore scope and impact across the organization (pain chain)
3. Guide the customer to envision the resolved state
4. Quantify value (ROI/TCO analysis)
5. Map buying committee and align solution with each stakeholder's success criteria
6. Propose and close

### When to use

Complex solutions that require customization. Markets where the customer doesn't know exactly what they need. Multi-stakeholder environments where different buyers have different definitions of success.

## Methodology selection guide

| Situation | Primary methodology | Complementary |
|---|---|---|
| Complex enterprise, long cycles | MEDDPICC (qualification) | Challenger (engagement approach) |
| Customer unaware of their problem | Challenger (teach and reframe) | SPIN (structured questioning) |
| Consultative, relationship-heavy | Sandler (mutual respect) | Solution Selling (vision creation) |
| Competitive market, well-informed buyers | Challenger + MEDDPICC | SPIN for discovery phase |
| Custom/configurable solutions | Solution Selling | MEDDPICC for qualification |
| Startup selling to enterprises | SPIN (discovery) + MEDDPICC (qualification) | Challenger for differentiation |
| Need to improve forecast accuracy | MEDDPICC | — |
| High churn, long negotiation cycles | Sandler (qualify out early) | MEDDPICC |

## Pipeline management

### Standard pipeline stages

| Stage | Description | Typical actions |
|---|---|---|
| **Prospecting** | Identifying potential customers | Outreach, inbound qualification, referrals |
| **Discovery** | Understanding the customer's situation and needs | SPIN questions, problem interviews, pain mapping |
| **Qualification** | Determining fit and likelihood of purchase | MEDDPICC scoring, budget/authority/need/timeline |
| **Demo / Proposal** | Presenting the solution | Tailored demo, proposal, pricing |
| **Negotiation** | Aligning terms and overcoming objections | Commercial terms, legal review, champion coaching |
| **Closed Won** | Deal signed | Handoff to implementation, post-sell (Sandler) |
| **Closed Lost** | Deal lost | Win/loss analysis, relationship maintenance |

### Key metrics

| Metric | Formula / Definition | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Win rate** | Closed Won / (Closed Won + Closed Lost) | Overall sales effectiveness |
| **Conversion rate** | Opportunities advancing between stages | Stage-level health; identifies bottlenecks |
| **Average deal size** | Total revenue / number of deals | Revenue planning, pipeline sizing |
| **Sales cycle length** | Average days from first contact to close | Forecasting, process optimization |
| **Deal velocity** | (Opportunities × Win Rate × Avg Deal Size) / Cycle Length | Revenue throughput of the pipeline |
| **Pipeline coverage** | Pipeline value / Quota | Health indicator (typically 3–4x coverage needed) |
| **Forecast accuracy** | Forecasted revenue vs actual revenue | Qualification rigor, prediction quality |

### Pipeline health rules of thumb

- **3–4x pipeline coverage** is healthy for most B2B sales orgs
- Conversion rates dropping stage-over-stage suggest a qualification problem
- Deals stalling in one stage indicate a process gap (missing champion, unclear decision process, unresolved objection)
- Increasing cycle length signals market headwind or qualification weakness
- Pipeline coverage without quality is vanity — high coverage with low win rates wastes resources

## Decision heuristics

- **"We have a great product but can't close"** → likely a qualification problem. Apply MEDDPICC rigorously
- **"Customers like us but buy from competitors"** → Challenger approach — teach something new, don't just build relationships
- **"Deals die at the last stage"** → Paper Process (MEDDPICC P) not qualified early enough; or no real champion
- **"Long sales cycles eating resources"** → Sandler — qualify harder, qualify earlier. "No" is a good outcome
- **"We keep discounting to win"** → value not established. Use SPIN Implication questions to make the cost of inaction visible; use Challenger to reframe value
- **"Different reps use different approaches"** → standardize on one methodology, customize to market. Challenger + MEDDPICC is a common combination

## Anti-patterns

- **Feature dumping** — listing product capabilities instead of connecting to customer pain; all methodologies agree this is wrong
- **Happy ears** — hearing what you want to hear in customer responses; interpreting politeness as buying signals
- **Single-threaded deal** — only one contact in the prospect organization; if they leave or lose influence, the deal dies
- **Premature demo** — showing the product before understanding the problem; Solution Selling and Sandler both prohibit this
- **Forecast fiction** — reporting pipeline values without qualification rigor; MEDDPICC exists to prevent this
- **Relationship-only selling** — relying on rapport without bringing insight; Challenger research shows this underperforms in complex sales
- **No win/loss analysis** — closing deals (or losing them) without understanding why; misses systematic improvement opportunities
- **Champion confusion** — mistaking a friendly contact for a champion; a real champion has power, access, vested interest, and credibility
